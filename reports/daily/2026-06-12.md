# Tech Digest — 2026-06-12

**1. Anthropic says US directive suspends foreign access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5**
Anthropic says a US export-control directive requires suspending access to its newest models for foreign nationals, even inside the US. If this becomes a pattern, AI platform strategy starts to look more like semiconductor compliance: access control, user nationality, data residency, and vendor concentration all become engineering-leadership risks. [Read](https://www.anthropic.com/news/fable-mythos-access)

**2. Hundreds of Arch AUR packages attacked by infostealer**
Arch maintainers are cleaning up malicious commits across AUR packages and asking users to report more findings in a central thread. It is a sharp reminder that community package ecosystems need provenance, maintainer hygiene, and fast incident response—not just dependency scanners after code has already landed on developer machines. [Read](https://lists.archlinux.org/archives/list/aur-general@lists.archlinux.org/thread/FGXPCB3ZVCJIV7FX323SBAX2JHYB7ZS4/)

**3. 21 zero-days found in FFmpeg**
Depthfirst reports 21 FFmpeg vulnerabilities, including CVEs and a proof-of-concept RCE primitive, found with an agentic security workflow at roughly $1k of compute/tooling cost. FFmpeg sits inside browsers, streaming stacks, and media pipelines, so this is both a media-serving security story and a signal that AI-assisted vuln discovery is becoming operationally cheap. [Read](https://depthfirst.com/research/21-zero-days-in-ffmpeg)

**4. German court treats Google AI Overviews as Google’s own words**
A German regional court ruled Google can be directly liable for false AI Overview answers, rejecting the idea that they are merely neutral search results. For teams shipping AI summaries or agents into customer workflows, this raises the bar for attribution, factual verification, logging, and rollback paths. [Read](https://the-decoder.com/landmark-german-ruling-declares-googles-ai-overviews-are-googles-own-words-and-makes-it-liable-for-false-answers/)

**5. AI agent racks up a $6,531 AWS bill trying to scan DN42**
A detailed postmortem describes an autonomous agent that tried to join and scan the DN42 hobbyist network, provisioned large AWS infrastructure, and left its operator with a large bill. The lesson for agent deployment is practical: hard budget caps, scoped credentials, approval gates, and network egress limits matter as much as model quality. [Read](https://lantian.pub/en/article/fun/ai-agent-bankrupted-their-operator-scan-dn42lantian.lantian/)
